            <h2 class="section-title">1. Introduction</h2>

            <p class="text-gray-700 leading-relaxed">

                This document outlines the framework for establishing and maturing the Internal Audit Investigations Program. While operating as an integral part of Uber's broader corporate investigations framework, this program also adheres strictly to IIA standards of conduct, reflecting its unique position within the Internal Audit team.

            </p>

            <p class="text-gray-700 leading-relaxed mt-4">

                A key focus of this program is to evolve from a primarily reactive stance to a more balanced and proactive approach to identifying and addressing misconduct. This involves enhancing our case intake mechanisms through improved collaboration with key internal stakeholders and expanding automated monitoring systems. Furthermore, we aim to establish a robust and systematic process for collecting, analysing, and acting upon identified root causes and issues, ensuring effective and consistent remediation.

            </p>

            <p class="text-gray-700 leading-relaxed mt-4">

                Leveraging emerging technologies, particularly AI, is central to these improvements. AI tools are being explored for tasks such as automated contract review and comprehensive report reading, promising to significantly enhance our investigative capabilities and efficiency.

            </p>

            <p class="text-gray-700 leading-relaxed mt-4">

                The Internal Audit Investigations Program is essential for safeguarding company assets, fostering a strong culture of integrity, and reinforcing confidence across the organisation.

            </p>

        </section>

            <h2 class="section-title">2. Key Components of the Program</h2>


            <!-- 2.1 Governance -->

            <div class="mb-4">

                <div class="collapsible-header" onclick="toggleCollapsible('governance')">

                    <h3>2.1. Governance</h3>

                    <span class="arrow" id="arrow-governance">&#9658;</span>

                </div>

                <div id="governance" class="collapsible-content hidden">

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">The Internal Audit investigations program is dual-governed. It is part of Uber’s wider corporate investigations program; but so long as it sits within the Internal Audit team, it is a function carried out by Internal Audit and therefore subject to IIA standards of conduct.</p>

                    <h4 class="subsection-title">Key requirements of Uber’s investigations program are:</h4>

                    <ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-gray-700">

                        <li>General conduct: Investigations are conducted in a fair and neutral manner</li>

                        <li>Planning: Investigations are adequately scoped and planned</li>

                        <li>Findings: Investigation findings must be based on information that is relevant, reliable, and reasonably sufficient to support the conclusions and/or recommendations</li>

                    </ul>

                    <h4 class="subsection-title">Key features of the IIA standards that apply to investigations are:</h4>

                    <ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-gray-700">

                        <li>Ethics and professionalism: Internal auditors must demonstrate integrity, maintain objectivity, demonstrate competency, exercise due professional case, and maintain confidentiality</li>

                        <li>Planning: A properly resourced and positioned internal audit function develops and implements a strategy to support the organisation’s success</li>

                        <li>Performance: Performing internal audit services requires internal auditors to effectively plan engagements, conduct the engagement work to develop findings and conclusions, and collaborate with management to identify recommendations and/or action plans that address the findings</li>

                        <li>Root cause analysis: When evaluating potential engagement findings, internal auditors must collaborate with management to identify the root causes where possible</li>

                    </ul>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Though part of the Finance org and with no direct management line through the Chief Ethics, Compliance and Security Officer who owns the overall investigations program, the Internal Audit investigations program must ensure conformance with both sets of standards, and close collaboration with counterpart personnel across the investigations portfolio, as well as within Internal Audit.</p>

                </div>

            </div>

            <!-- 2.3 Case Intake -->

            <div class="mb-4">

                <div class="collapsible-header" onclick="toggleCollapsible('caseIntake')">

                    <h3>2.3. Case Intake</h3>

                    <span class="arrow" id="arrow-caseIntake">&#9658;</span>

                </div>

                <div id="caseIntake" class="collapsible-content hidden">

                    <div class="key-takeaway">

                        <h4>Key Takeaway</h4>

                        <p>To enhance proactive detection, the program needs improved collaboration with key internal teams and an expansion of automated transaction monitoring systems.</p>

                    </div>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Present state</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">The Internal Audit Investigations program is currently largely reactive, and (with some exceptions) dependent upon integrity hotline reports.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Desired future state</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">It is intended to develop the program towards a more balanced proactive / reactive footing.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">According to the ACFE Report To The Nations 2024, while tips are the leading single cause of organisational fraud discovery, they are responsible for fewer than half of all detections overall. Other high-impact detection methods are internal audit, management review, document examination, account reconciliation, and by accident.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">In some recent cases, other teams (including Strategic Sourcing and Strategic Finance) have developed concerns about potential fraud matters, but have not consulted investigations personnel (or have consulted investigations team months after the fact). Ongoing collaboration and outreach to stakeholders in key teams, including Strategic Sourcing and Strategic Finance, may improve the pipeline of information and lead to more proactive investigations.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Ongoing transaction monitoring in fields such as Travel and Expense and vendor transactions does take place throughout Uber, but Internal Audit has very little visibility over it, and much of it is geared primarily towards budgetary compliance and not necessarily with fraud and misconduct in mind. There may be opportunities to improve, re-focus, and build upon existing monitoring mechanisms to identify investigation opportunities. One example: based on recent cases, use an AI reader across Uber’s vendor contract repository to identify contracts that contain provisions for vendors to hold pots of Uber’s money off-book.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Data is available about the quantity and type of ethics hotline reports per country. This data may be leveraged to identify countries where Uber has large numbers of employees, but low engagement with the hotline. These countries may be suitable for further proactive engagement including site visits and discussions with personnel on high-risk teams. Further ways to analyse and leverage investigation data will also be explored with the Ethics and Compliance team.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Regional Ethics and Compliance teams go so far as to conduct on-site roadshows to proactively engage with personnel in under-reporting areas. The IA investigations lead will discuss this activity with regional leads, and explore the potential upside of conducting similar activities in parallel.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Action points</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-gray-800">

                        <li>Identify key stakeholders within StratFin and StratSourcing for periodic ongoing engagement</li>

                        <li>Work with T&E team to gain better visibility over travel and expense transaction monitoring</li>

                        <li>Identify opportunities for improvement and and expansion of the same</li>

                        <li>Work with StratFin and StratSourcing to gain better visibility over vendor transaction monitoring</li>

                        <li>Identify opportunities for improvement and expansion of the same</li>

                        <li>Work with legal (primarily Kelly Schulz) to explore use of AI in contracts (and develop other opportunities)</li>

                        <li>Work with Kelly Schulz and Nell Glazener-Cooney to gather and analyse data relating to hotline reports around the world</li>

                        <li>Work with Samantha Boel and others to explore the benefits of on-site roadshows and other proactive outreach</li>

                    </ul>

                </div>

            </div>

                    <h3>2.4. Collaboration</h3>

                    <span class="arrow" id="arrow-collaboration">&#9658;</span>

                <div id="collaboration" class="collapsible-content hidden">

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Ongoing collaboration with other investigation teams is both a requirement of Uber’s Internal Investigations Protocol, and a matter of general good sense.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Meeting cadences specifically stipulated in the Protocol, to be attended by Alex, include</p>

                    <ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-gray-800">

                        <li>Bi-weekly Investigative Leads meetings (30 minutes, every 2 weeks)</li>

                        <li>Regional leads meetings (1 hour, every quarter, x4 for the four mega-regions)</li>

                        <li>Financial Risk Management meetings (1 hour, every quarter)</li>

                        <li>Other meetings on an as-needed basis, particularly when Internal Audit jointly investigates cases with other teams</li>

                    </ul>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">This amounts to a minimum of 8 hours of meetings per quarter, plus the same again to account for preparation and recording time.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Key stakeholders to be prioritised for ongoing collaboration efforts at this stage are Kevin White (the overall owner of the investigation program), and Kelly Schulz (the lead for Ethics and Compliance investigations and a key component of the program overall).</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Additionally, recent cases have indicated a disconnect between Internal Audit and some other investigative teams, particularly ER and HR; and a lack of mutual visibility and understanding of differing investigative scopes and timelines. Better collaboration and clearer expectation-setting is a priority going forward.</p>

            <!-- 2.6 Root cause analysis and remediation -->

            <div class="mb-4">

                <div class="collapsible-header" onclick="toggleCollapsible('rootCauseRemediation')">

                    <h3>2.6. Root cause analysis and remediation</h3>

                    <span class="arrow" id="arrow-rootCauseRemediation">&#9658;</span>

                </div>

                <div id="rootCauseRemediation" class="collapsible-content hidden">

                    <div class="key-takeaway">

                        <h4>Key Takeaway</h4>

                        <p>A more systematic approach is needed to collect, analyse, and act upon root causes and identified issues from investigations to ensure effective remediation.</p>

                    </div>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Present state</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">Although conducting root cause analysis, and remediating identified issues, are expectations across Uber’s investigation teams, in practice these activities are inconsistently and haphazardly carried out.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Desired future state</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">The Internal Audit investigations lead is uniquely positioned to take on a pivotal role in the analysis and remediation of identified issues. Identifying those issues, achieving buy-in from responsible stakeholders, developing action plans, assigning owners, and tracking progress against set milestones are all well within ordinary Internal Audit activities. Those activities, and the processes and mechanisms that underpin them, require very little tweaking to be put to investigative use.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">The missing link in the process as it exists today is a systematic way of gathering the right data from investigations, extracting root causes, and collating and prioritising remediation activity upon which those processes can be brought to bear. It is proposed that the IA investigations lead takes primary responsibility for this part of the process.</p>

                    <br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800">As a starting point, it is proposed that investigation reports be used as the primary documentary repository of root cause analysis. The extent to which this analysis is properly conducted in cases where it is warranted may be reviewed as this project develops. Whether any coaching or reinforcement in this area is required may be discussed with the leadership of the investigation teams as and when the need arises. Those reports should be read and digested - either by the IA investigations lead or, ideally and if the technology is sufficiently capable and reliable, by AI (or by some combination of the two) - and persistent root causes and opportunities for remediation drawn out. These may be discussed and agreed with other investigation leads on already-existing meeting cadences. Once agreed for action, the IA investigations lead will be responsible for leveraging Internal Audit processes for developing and monitoring action plans, and likewise reporting back to investigations leadership.</p>

                    <br><br>

                    <p class="font-medium text-gray-800"><span class="underline">Action points</span></p>

                    <br>

                    <ul class="list-disc ml-6 text-gray-800">

                        <li>IA investigations lead to secure buy-in for this suggested remediations process from investigations leadership</li>

                        <li>IA investigations lead to secure support from Internal Audit leadership</li>

                        <li>IA investigations lead and Ethics and Compliance investigations lead to devise a mechanism for collecting, reviewing and prioritising remediation actions (whether using AI or otherwise) from investigation reports</li>

                    </ul>

                </div>
